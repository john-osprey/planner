# Family Midwinter Training Project

**Status**: Active
**Start Date**: 2025-11-30
**Training Period**: December 2025 - March 2026 (Midwinter)
**Priority**: High
**Project Type**: Family development, fitness, relationship building

## Project Overview

Create a structured midwinter training program that brings Dad (46, Boston Marathon training) and boys (ages 12 and 10, baseball offseason) together through shared fitness goals, age-appropriate training, regular dialogue, and quality time. Build good habits, set positive examples, and create lasting memories through shared commitment to improvement.

## Core Objectives

1. **Individual Excellence**: Each person achieves their training goals
2. **Shared Experience**: Regular training activities done together
3. **Open Dialogue**: Weekly check-ins about training, challenges, and progress
4. **Habit Building**: Establish sustainable fitness routines for all
5. **Quality Time**: Use training as opportunity to connect and bond
6. **Positive Modeling**: Dad demonstrates commitment, perseverance, and balance

## Training Participants

### Dad (John, Age 46)

- **Background**: Elite runner (college runner, 2:29 marathon PR)

- **Weekly Volume**: Building toward 50-70 miles/week (marathon-specific)

### Older Son (Age 12)
- **Goal**: Baseball offseason conditioning, build athletic foundation
- **Sport**: Travel baseball
- **Focus**: General athleticism, strength, speed, endurance base

### Younger Son (Age 10)

- **Sport**: Travel baseball
- **Focus**: Movement skills, coordination, building fitness base

## Age-Appropriate Training Guidelines

### For 12-Year-Old
**Running:**
- Max 3-4 runs per week
- Duration: 15-25 minutes maximum
- Pace: Conversational, easy effort (can talk comfortably)
- No speedwork or intense intervals
- Focus on enjoyment, not performance

**Strength:**
- Bodyweight exercises only (no weights yet)
- 2-3 sessions per week, 15-20 minutes
- Focus on form and control, not reps
- Exercises: Push-ups, pull-ups (assisted OK), planks, squats, lunges
- Keep it fun and varied

**Rest:**
- Minimum 2-3 complete rest days per week
- Listen to body, skip if tired or sore
- Sleep is priority (9-11 hours/night)

### For 10-Year-Old
**Running:**
- Max 2-3 runs per week
- Duration: 10-20 minutes maximum
- Pace: Very easy, always conversational
- Make it playful (exploring, games, following trails)
- Never forced, always optional

**Strength:**
- Bodyweight exercises, game-based
- 1-2 sessions per week, 10-15 minutes
- Focus on fun movements and coordination
- Exercises: Modified push-ups, planks, squats, animal movements
- Stop if loses interest

**Rest:**
- Minimum 3-4 complete rest days per week
- Zero pressure to participate every time
- Sleep is critical (9-12 hours/night)

### For Dad (46, Marathon Training)
**Running:**
- 5-6 days per week
- Weekly volume: Building 40-70 miles
- Mix of easy runs, tempo, long runs, speedwork
- Focused marathon preparation

**Strength:**
- 2-3 sessions per week
- Core work, bodyweight, injury prevention
- Can do heavier work separately, bodyweight with boys

**Recovery:**
- Quality sleep, nutrition, injury prevention
- Model good recovery practices for boys

## Shared Training Activities

### 1. Sunday Morning Family Easy Runs
**Frequency**: Weekly (weather permitting)
**Duration**: 10-20 minutes for boys, Dad continues after
**Format**:
- Start together, very easy conversational pace
- Boys set the pace (Dad holds back)
- Explore neighborhood, find new routes
- Make it fun - count dogs, spot holiday lights, tell stories
- Boys finish 10-20 minutes, Dad continues his long run

### 2. Tuesday/Thursday Evening Bodyweight Strength
**Frequency**: 2x per week
**Duration**: 15-20 minutes total
**Format**:
- Living room or basement
- Dad leads but participates
- Circuit style, rotate through exercises
- Celebrate effort, not perfection
- Sample circuit (3 rounds):
  - 10 push-ups (modified as needed)
  - 15 squats
  - 20-second plank
  - 10 lunges each leg
  - 10 sit-ups
  - 30-second rest

### 3. Saturday Morning Training Check-In
**Frequency**: Weekly
**Duration**: 15-30 minutes
**Format**:
- Casual breakfast or hot chocolate
- Each person shares:
  - How training felt this week
  - What went well
  - What was challenging
  - Goals for next week
- Dad shares his training too (model transparency)
- Celebrate small wins
- Problem-solve challenges together

## Conversation Framework

### Weekly Discussion Topics
- **Week 1-2**: Setting personal goals, what motivates us
- **Week 3-4**: Dealing with hard days, staying consistent
- **Week 5-6**: Nutrition and fueling, what makes us feel good
- **Week 7-8**: Sleep and recovery, why rest matters
- **Week 9-10**: Progress check, celebrating improvements
- **Week 11-12**: Handling setbacks, mental toughness

### Questions to Ask Boys
- "What part of training did you enjoy most this week?"
- "Was anything harder than you expected?"
- "How do you feel when you finish a workout?"
- "What goal do you want to work on next week?"

### What Dad Should Share

- How you handle bad runs or tired days
- Why you love running, what it gives you
- Times you wanted to quit but didn't
- How training makes you feel accomplished

## Important Principles

### 1. **Never Force Participation**
- If a boy doesn't want to do a session, that's OK
- Make it invitational, not mandatory
- Keep it positive and pressure-free

### 2. **Prioritize Fun Over Performance**
- No times, no races, no comparisons (especially between brothers)
- Celebrate effort and consistency, not speed or reps
- Keep variety and playfulness in activities

### 3. **Model, Don't Preach**
- Let boys see your commitment through actions
- Share your process honestly (good and bad days)
- Show that adults train too, it's lifelong

### 4. **Listen More Than Talk**
- Ask questions, hear their experiences
- Validate their feelings (tired, sore, proud, frustrated)
- Let them guide the conversation

### 5. **Adjust as Needed**
- If something isn't working, change it
- Scale back if anyone seems burned out
- Increase if they're loving it and asking for more

### 6. **Safety First**
- Watch for injury signs (limping, consistent pain)
- Ensure proper warm-up before activities
- Don't push through pain
- Prioritize sleep and nutrition

## Contingencies

### Bad Weather
- Indoor bodyweight sessions
- Mall walking or indoor track
- Make it optional for boys
- Dad uses treadmill if needed

### Illness/Injury
- Immediate rest for affected person
- Others continue (model independence)
- Return gradually, no rush
- Seek medical advice if needed

### Loss of Interest
- Take a week off, reassess
- Ask boys what would make it fun again
- Simplify or modify program
- Remember: building relationship > perfect training

### Schedule Conflicts
- Be flexible, reschedule sessions
- Family activities take priority sometimes
- Consistency matters more than perfection
- Miss workouts without guilt
